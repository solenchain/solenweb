Why Solen

Solen is a next-generation blockchain built for speed, simplicity, and real-world usability. While networks like Ethereum pioneered the idea of programmable money, they were designed over a decade ago and carry the weight of that legacy — slow transactions, unpredictable fees that can spike to $50 or more, and a user experience that feels like it was built for engineers, not everyday people. Solen processes transactions in seconds for a fraction of a penny, making it practical for everything from micropayments to large-scale applications without worrying about cost.

One of Solen's most powerful innovations is smart accounts. On traditional blockchains, if you lose your private key, your funds are gone forever — there's no customer support to call, no reset button. Solen changes that with built-in account recovery through trusted guardians, the ability to sign in using your fingerprint or Face ID through passkeys, and features like session keys that let apps act on your behalf with strict limits. It brings the kind of safety net people expect from modern banking into the world of crypto.

Solen also features a native intent system that fundamentally changes how you interact with the blockchain. Instead of manually navigating through multiple steps, approvals, and transactions, you simply express what you want — "swap these tokens at the best price" or "bridge my funds to another network" — and specialized solvers compete to fulfill your request in the most efficient way possible. You get better outcomes without needing to understand the technical details underneath.

For developers and businesses, Solen offers native rollup support, allowing anyone to launch their own dedicated blockchain that settles back to Solen's secure base layer. This means a gaming company, a financial platform, or a social network can each have their own high-performance chain tailored to their needs, while still benefiting from Solen's shared security and the ability to move assets seamlessly between them.

Finally, Solen is governed by its community. Token holders vote on protocol upgrades, fee parameters, and network policies directly on-chain — no backroom deals or foundation overrides. Combined with a transparent staking system where validators are held accountable through automatic slashing for misbehavior, Solen is built to be a network that its users truly own and trust.

Architecture

Solen is built as a modular stack written entirely in Rust, where each layer has a clear responsibility and can evolve independently. At the top, execution domains — rollups, app chains, and sovereign environments — run their own logic and submit settlement proofs down to the base layer. They never compete with each other for throughput because each domain operates in its own execution space.

The heart of the system is the Solen L1 settlement layer. This is where consensus happens through a BFT proof-of-stake protocol with stake-weighted proposer selection, where smart accounts provide programmable authentication and recovery, where cross-domain bridge vaults secure assets moving between rollups, and where the intent engine matches user requests with competing solvers for optimal execution. A built-in WASM virtual machine executes smart contracts with fuel metering and deterministic execution guarantees.

Beneath it all, data availability ensures that every piece of state can be independently verified. Solen supports both on-chain data availability for maximum security and integration with external DA layers for applications that prioritize cost efficiency. The result is an architecture that scales horizontally through rollups while maintaining the security and finality guarantees of a unified settlement layer.

Staking

Validators earn epoch rewards from a 500M pool distributed over 10 years. Delegators share rewards proportional to stake. 7-epoch unbonding period.

Fee Burn

50% of all transaction fees are burned permanently, creating deflationary pressure. At high volume, burns can exceed reward issuance.

Governance

Staked token holders vote on protocol parameters with 66.67% supermajority. 30% quorum required. Total supply cap requires a hard fork.
